Research: San Diego Supercomputer Center - Network Dynamics of Cancer Genomes

Cellular functions are maintained by a very delicate balance of chemicals through regulation of genes in negative feedback loops. When this regulation goes awry complications emerge. The mutations in the specific genes can affect all functions of the cell. The effects of these changes are seen in cancer as a change in gene regulation of major tumor suppressor genes and oncogenes. The complexity of the molecular interactions in a cell can be modeled and computed in several different ways. Rietman et al. use data on concentrations of proteins from The Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA) to calculate Gibbs Free Energy, a thermodynamic property of protein-protein interaction (PPI) networks. They found that there was a correlation between a lower Gibbs Free Energy cancer and a higher 5-year survival rate. We analyzed several such cancer networks from KEGG Pathways for measures of various network parameters including entropy and their relation to gene regulation. We found that cancers with more complex gene interactions have lower survival rates. This is important to the future of personalized cancer therapy as it can help identify new genes to target for drug development and better treat cancers.

This research was completed with Dr. Igor Tsigelny Ph.D. at University of California, San Diego.
